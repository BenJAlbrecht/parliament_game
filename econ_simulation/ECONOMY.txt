# Game Economy — v2 Specification

## Overview

The economy simulates a closed national economy each turn (1 turn = 1 quarter).
It tracks GDP and its three components, plus unemployment, inflation, and the
central bank interest rate. Player actions (passing bills) change government
spending and tax rates, which propagate through the model to produce quarterly
economic outcomes. The central bank responds to those outcomes with a one-quarter
lag, adjusting the interest rate according to a Taylor Rule.

The model is built from four classical macro relationships:
- The Keynesian cross (GDP from aggregate demand)
- Okun's law (output gap → unemployment)
- The Phillips curve (output gap → inflation)
- The Taylor Rule (inflation and unemployment → central bank rate)

Plus a stochastic shock to private investment (business-sentiment variability),
three exogenous shock vectors that can represent crises, supply disruptions, or
cost pressures, and two structural policy levers that raise trend growth or heal
accumulated scarring over time.

All rates (g, π_target, i_neutral) are expressed in per-quarter terms internally.
Player-facing text always presents these in annualized terms — the central bank
"targets 2% annual inflation," not "0.496% quarterly inflation."

## Identity and equations

GDP is the sum of consumption, investment, and government spending:

    Y = C + I + G

Each component:

    C = a + mpc · (Y − T)
    I = I_base · trend_t · (1 − τ · (tax_rate − tax_baseline)) ·
                           (1 − d · (i − i_neutral)) · (1 + ε_t) · (1 + δ_t)
    G = G_path[t] · trend_t
    T = tax_rate · Y

Where:
- a = a_0 · trend_t · (1 + δ_t) is trend-scaled autonomous consumption,
  also affected by demand shocks
- trend_t is a cumulative trend factor that starts at 1 in quarter 1 and compounds
  each quarter at the current growth rate: trend_{t+1} = trend_t · (1 + g_current_t).
  With no structural boosts, trend_t = (1 + g)^(t−1). Structural growth reforms
  raise g_current permanently, so trend accelerates from the quarter after each reform.
- ε_t ~ N(0, σ²) is the investment shock for quarter t
- δ_t = demand_shock_path[t] is a demand shock applied to both a and I
- i is the central bank interest rate set by the Taylor Rule (see below)
- G_path[t] is the player's real (quarter-1-equivalent) spending choice for quarter t

Y is solved analytically from these (since C depends on Y):

    Y = (a + I + G) / (1 − mpc · (1 − tax_rate))

## Mandatory vs. discretionary spending

G_path[t] represents the government's real spending level for quarter t, expressed
in quarter-1-equivalent terms. The model automatically scales it up by trend growth:

    G_t = G_path[t] · trend_t

This reflects how most government spending is mandatory or entitlement-driven —
pensions, healthcare, statutory benefits — and grows automatically with
population aging, indexation, and demographic pressures, roughly tracking the
economy's trend growth rate. The player does not need to legislate to maintain
this baseline; it is built in.

Bills, therefore, represent real changes to spending priorities above or below
the trend-adjusted baseline. A bill raising G_path from 270 to 285 commits the
government to 5.6% more real spending every quarter from that point forward, not
a one-time nominal boost.

When the player does nothing (G_path constant), G grows in line with I, a, and
Y*, and the economy stays near potential output. The player's legislative choices
determine whether the economy runs above or below potential — not whether it
survives at all.

**Future extension.** Splitting G into a mandatory component (grows at g
automatically) and a discretionary component (set directly by the player) is
a planned refinement. For now, all spending is treated as real and trend-scaled.

## Output gap, unemployment, inflation

Potential output grows at the trend rate, modified by any supply shock and
accumulated hysteresis scarring:

    Y*_t = Y*_0 · trend_t · (1 + σ_t) · scar_t

where σ_t = supply_shock_path[t] and scar_t is the cumulative scar factor
(see Hysteresis / scarring below). The output gap measures the economy relative
to potential:

    gap = (Y − Y*) / Y*

When positive, the economy runs hot (above potential). When negative, slack
(below potential).

Okun's law translates the output gap into unemployment:

    u = u_natural − okun · gap

A hot economy → unemployment below natural rate. A slack economy → unemployment
above natural rate.

The Phillips curve translates the output gap into inflation, with an optional
cost-push term:

    π = π_target + β · gap + ζ_t

where ζ_t = inflation_shock_path[t]. A hot economy generates above-target
inflation; a slack economy produces below-target inflation or deflation.
Cost-push shocks add to π regardless of the output gap.

β = 0.25 reflects a modern, relatively flat Phillips curve consistent with
OECD post-1990 empirical estimates (typical range 0.1–0.2). A steeper slope
(0.4–0.6) would model a pre-Volcker / 1970s economy where wage-price spirals
were more responsive to slack and overheating.

## Central bank

The central bank has a dual mandate: keep inflation near its 2% annual target
and unemployment near u_natural (5%). Internally, π_target = 0.00496 per quarter
(0.496% per quarter ≈ 2% annualized). All player-facing text expresses this
mandate in annual terms: "the independent central bank targets 2% annual inflation
and full employment."

The CB sets the interest rate each quarter using a Taylor Rule with interest rate
smoothing, reacting to the previous quarter's outcomes with a one-quarter lag.

First, a Taylor target is computed from last quarter's inflation and unemployment:

    i_target = i_neutral + φ_π · (π_{t-1} − π_target) + φ_u · (u_natural − u_{t-1})

Then the actual rate moves only part of the way toward that target, with
asymmetric smoothing depending on the direction of the move:

    i_t = rho · i_{t-1} + (1 - rho) · i_target

    where rho = rho_cut  = 0.9   if i_target < i_{t-1}   (easing:     10% per quarter)
               rho_hike = 0.8   if i_target >= i_{t-1}  (tightening: 20% per quarter)

When tightening, the CB closes 20% of the gap to its Taylor target each quarter.
When easing, it closes only 10% — moving half as fast toward lower rates as it
does toward higher rates. This models a credibility-focused, hawkish CB that is
quick to fight inflation but cautious about cutting rates prematurely.

The interest rate feeds back into private investment through the sensitivity
parameter d:

    (1 − d · (i − i_neutral))

When i > i_neutral (tightening), this factor is below 1 and suppresses
investment. When i < i_neutral (easing), it is above 1 and boosts investment.

**Initialisation.** For quarter 1, π_{0} = π_target, u_{0} = u_natural, and
i_{0} = i_neutral, so i_1 = i_neutral. The CB starts at neutral and only
deviates once the economy generates an output gap.

**Zero lower bound.** After asymmetric smoothing, the policy rate is clipped at
zero:

    i_t = max(0, rho · i_{t-1} + (1 - rho) · i_target)

The CB cannot set a negative nominal rate. Once i_t hits zero the Taylor Rule
becomes inoperative: further deterioration in inflation or unemployment no longer
lowers the rate. The economy loses its automatic CB stabiliser and fiscal policy
becomes the only remaining lever.

## Shocks

Three exogenous shock vectors run alongside G_path and tax_path, all defaulting
to zero. They are passed as arguments to simulate_economy() and have the same
length as G_path.

### Demand shock (demand_shock_path)

A demand shock represents a simultaneous collapse of private spending intentions
across both households and businesses — a financial crisis, a consumer-confidence
crash, or a broad credit crunch. It applies multiplicatively to both autonomous
consumption and investment:

    a_t = a_0 · (1 + g)^(t−1) · (1 + δ_t)
    I_t = I_base · ... · (1 + ε_t) · (1 + δ_t)

Routing the shock through both a and I is economically honest: a real demand
collapse suppresses both household spending intentions and business investment
plans simultaneously. Hitting only investment would miss the consumer-confidence
channel; hitting only consumption would miss the business-sentiment channel.

**Recession signature:** deflationary slump — Y falls, unemployment rises,
inflation drops below target. The CB responds with rate cuts, but the one-quarter
lag and the slower easing path (rho_cut = 0.9: only 10% per quarter) mean
relief arrives slowly — slower than tightening would in the mirror scenario.

Example — two-quarter financial crisis (quarters 4–5):
    demand_shock_path[4:5] <- -0.15

### Supply shock (supply_shock_path)

A supply shock compresses the economy's productive capacity without directly
cutting private demand — an oil embargo, a pandemic, a natural disaster. It
shifts potential output downward while actual Y may remain elevated, pushing
the output gap positive despite a weakening real economy:

    Y*_t = Y*_0 · trend_t · (1 + σ_t)

**Recession signature:** stagflation — actual Y holds up while Y* falls, so
the output gap stays positive or turns positive. Unemployment may fall even as
the real economy weakens in absolute terms. Inflation rises above target. The CB
faces a dilemma: raising rates to fight inflation tightens an already-strained
economy.

Example — 1970s-style supply compression (quarters 9–28, approximately 5 years):
    supply_shock_path[9:28] <- -0.08

### Cost-push shock (inflation_shock_path)

A cost-push shock adds directly to observed inflation without moving the output
gap — an import price spike, a commodity surge, a supply-chain disruption that
raises prices without reducing real output:

    π_t = π_target + β · gap + ζ_t

**Recession signature:** CB dilemma — inflation is above target but the real
economy may be at or below potential. Raising rates to fight cost-push inflation
induces unnecessary unemployment; ignoring it risks unanchored expectations.

Example — three-quarter commodity price surge (quarters 5–7):
    inflation_shock_path[5:7] <- 0.03

### Multi-quarter recessions

A single-quarter shock recovers quickly: the CB responds in t+1, investment
rebounds in t+2, and the output gap closes within two to three quarters. To model
a sustained recession, apply the shock across multiple consecutive quarters:

- 2008-style financial crisis:  demand_shock_path[13:21] <- -0.12
- COVID shock:                  demand_shock_path[1:4] <- -0.20
- 1970s stagflation combo:      supply_shock_path[9:28] <- -0.08
                                inflation_shock_path[9:28] <- 0.05

## Hysteresis / scarring

Prolonged negative output gaps permanently damage potential output — a mechanism
known as hysteresis. Skills atrophy when workers are unemployed for extended
periods. Capital depreciates without reinvestment. Discouraged workers exit the
labour force permanently. The result is a lower structural ceiling for the
economy even after the original shock has passed.

The model tracks this via a stateful scar factor that multiplicatively erodes Y*:

    scar_{t+1} = scar_t · (1 + min(0, gap_t) · hysteresis_rate)

Negative gaps shrink the scar factor; positive gaps (overheating) leave it
unchanged. The ratchet is strictly one-way: Y* can never recover lost ground
through the scar factor alone. Once potential output is permanently reduced,
only structural policy (modelled separately) could reverse it.

**Scarring is gap-based, not shock-based.** The mechanism fires whenever the
output gap is negative, regardless of what caused it. A demand shock scars
directly: Y falls while Y* holds, gap turns negative, scarring accumulates.
A supply shock scars indirectly: Y* falls, inflation rises, the CB tightens,
investment drops, Y eventually falls below the new (lower) Y*, and the negative
gap then scars further. A cost-push shock scars in the same indirect way:
CB tightening to fight above-gap inflation can create a negative gap that scars.

**The 2008 textbook case.** The 2008 financial crisis was a demand-side shock.
GDP recovered within a few years in most economies, but productivity growth
slowed permanently. Economists attribute this to capital underinvestment,
R&D cuts, and long-term unemployment that destroyed human capital. The scar
factor models exactly this channel: the recession itself, not the initial
shock, is what left economies permanently smaller.

**Gameplay implication.** Aggressive recession response — spending to close
negative gaps quickly — has a long-run payoff beyond the immediate stabilisation.
Every quarter a gap remains negative, scar_factor ticks down and Y* is permanently
reduced, compounding the damage over future turns. Conversely, a player who lets
a recession run its course faces a permanently smaller economy in the back half
of the session. The hysteresis mechanism gives players a reason to care about
speed of recovery, not just eventual recovery.

## Structural policies

Structural policies are long-run supply-side levers — distinct from short-run
demand management via G and tax_rate. They do not shift Y directly; they raise
the economy's ceiling (potential output) over time, so the same level of demand
produces less inflationary pressure and more sustainable growth.

Two mechanisms are available, passed as per-quarter vectors alongside the shock paths:

### Trend growth boost (g_boost_path)

Each quarter's value is permanently added to the running trend growth rate g_current:

    g_current_t = g_current_{t-1} + g_boost_path[t]

From the quarter after the boost, trend_t compounds at the higher g_current, so all
trend-scaled quantities (Y*, G, a, I) grow faster. The effect is permanent and
cumulative: a reform in quarter 5 raises the growth rate for all subsequent quarters.

    trend_{t+1} = trend_t · (1 + g_current_t)

Example bills and magnitudes:

| Reform                          | g_boost  |
|---------------------------------|----------|
| Public Education Investment     | +0.001   |
| Research and Innovation Fund    | +0.002   |
| Infrastructure Investment Act   | +0.001   |
| Immigration Expansion           | +0.0015  |

**Compounding over decades.** Small g_boost values produce large effects over
long horizons. A +0.001 boost held for 120 quarters (30 years) raises trend_t by
roughly 3% above the no-reform baseline. A +0.002 boost for 120 quarters adds ~6%.
Because the boost is multiplicative and permanent, early reforms compound longer
and are worth more than late reforms.

### Scar healing (heal_path)

Each quarter's value is added directly to scar_factor, capped at 1.0:

    scar_factor_after_healing = min(1.0, scar_factor + heal_path[t])

Healing is applied at the start of each turn, before Y_star is computed. It
immediately raises Y*, reducing the negative output gap that would otherwise
compound further scarring. At scar_factor = 1.0 (no damage), heal is a no-op.

Example bills and magnitudes:

| Reform                          | heal     |
|---------------------------------|----------|
| Workforce Retraining Initiative | +0.005   |
| Industrial Renewal Program      | +0.010   |
| Capital Investment Tax Credit   | +0.003   |

### Short-run vs. long-run policy

Demand-side tools (G, tax_rate) shift Y immediately but face CB crowding-out
over time. Structural tools shift Y* gradually, raising the ceiling without
triggering the CB. The optimal player strategy combines both: stabilise Y in
the short run with demand policy, invest in structural reforms to raise the
long-run ceiling, and use scar healing to recover from any recession damage
before hysteresis compounds it.

## Parameters

All rate parameters (g, π_target, i_neutral) are per-quarter internally.
Player-facing text annualizes them: the CB "targets 2% annual inflation."

| Parameter      | Value   | Meaning                                                  |
|----------------|---------|----------------------------------------------------------|
| mpc            | 0.75    | Marginal propensity to consume                           |
| a_0            | 80      | Autonomous consumption in quarter 1 (trend-scaled after) |
| τ              | 1.5     | Investment sensitivity to tax rate                       |
| tax_baseline   | 0.25    | Reference tax rate (no investment effect at this level)  |
| g              | 0.00496 | Baseline trend growth rate per quarter ((1.02)^(1/4)−1; ≈ 2% annualized) |
| σ (shock_sd)   | 0.03    | Std dev of investment shock                              |
| u_natural_baseline | 0.05 | Floor for natural rate of unemployment (u_natural_state starts here) |
| okun           | 0.5     | Okun coefficient (output gap → unemployment)             |
| π_target       | 0.00496 | Target inflation per quarter (≈ 2% annualized; CB mandate stated annually to player) |
| β              | 0.25    | Phillips slope (output gap → inflation) — flat, post-1990 OECD range |
| I_base_0       | 150     | Baseline investment in quarter 1                         |
| Y*_0           | ≈1142.86| Potential output in quarter 1 (auto-computed; see below) |
| i_neutral      | 0.005   | CB neutral interest rate per quarter (≈ 2% annualized)   |
| φ_π            | 1.5     | CB response to inflation gap (Taylor Rule)               |
| phi_u          | 0.25    | CB response to unemployment gap (Taylor Rule) — lower weight reflects price-stability priority |
| d              | 5       | Investment sensitivity to interest rate deviation        |
| rho_hike       | 0.8     | Rate smoothing when tightening (CB closes 20% of gap per quarter) |
| rho_cut        | 0.9     | Rate smoothing when easing (CB closes 10% of gap per quarter — slower) |
| hysteresis_rate| 0.10    | Fraction of negative gap that permanently scars Y*       |

**Y*_0 is auto-computed.** It is set equal to the quarter-1 equilibrium Y
(with no shocks and CB at neutral) so that the initial output gap is exactly
zero. With the defaults above and G_path[1] = 270, tax_path[1] = 0.25:

    Y*_0 = (a_0 + I_base_0 + G_0) / (1 − mpc · (1 − tax_baseline))
         = (80 + 150 + 270) / (1 − 0.75 · 0.75)
         = 500 / 0.4375
         ≈ 1142.86

Override by passing Y_star_0 in the params list if you need a specific value.

## Display conventions

Internal model values (Y, Y_star, C, I, G, rates) are kept in raw model units
and per-quarter rates throughout all calculations. The display layer converts
these to player-facing quantities. All conversion code lives in display.R.

### Normalization constants

| Constant        | Value          | Meaning                                         |
|-----------------|----------------|-------------------------------------------------|
| POPULATION      | 80,000,000     | Country population                              |
| BASE_GDP_CAPITA | 20,000 EUR     | GDP per capita at quarter 1                     |
| BASE_Y_MODEL    | 1,142.857      | Model Y at quarter-1 equilibrium (auto-computed)|
| PER_CAPITA_SCALE| ~17.5 EUR/unit | BASE_GDP_CAPITA / BASE_Y_MODEL                  |

These constants are locked in for worldbuilding consistency. Model Y at quarter 1
maps to exactly 20,000 EUR per capita for a population of 80 million. Aggregate
GDP at quarter 1 is therefore 80M x 20,000 = 1.6 trillion EUR — a plausible
mid-sized European economy.

### Calendar

Quarter 1 of the simulation corresponds to 1985 Q1. The conversion is:

    year(t) = 1985 + floor((t - 1) / 4)
    quarter_of_year(t) = ((t - 1) mod 4) + 1

A 30-year run (120 quarters) spans 1985 Q1 through 2014 Q4. Plots show year
labels at 5-year intervals (1985, 1990, 1995, ...) on the x-axis.

### Presentation rules

| Quantity                   | Display form           | Precision     |
|----------------------------|------------------------|---------------|
| GDP / Y_star / baseline Y* | EUR per capita         | Nearest EUR 100|
| C, I, G                    | % of quarterly GDP     | 0.1 pp        |
| Inflation (pi)             | Annualized, %          | 0.1 pp        |
| CB interest rate (i)       | Annualized, %          | 0.1 pp        |
| Trend growth rate (g)      | Annualized, %          | 0.1 pp        |
| Unemployment (u, u*)       | Level %, as-is         | 0.1 pp        |
| Scar factor                | Level (0–1)            | 3 decimal places|

Annualization uses compound formula: (1 + r_quarterly)^4 - 1. Annual inflation
target ~2.0%, annual neutral rate ~2.0% (both derived from 0.00496 and 0.005
quarterly defaults).

### Bills and spending units

When bills are presented to the player, spending changes will be denominated in
Euros. A bill that raises G_path by 15 (model units) translates to:

    15 x PER_CAPITA_SCALE x POPULATION = 15 x ~17.5 x 80M = ~21 billion EUR/quarter

Player-facing bill text will read "+21B EUR annual spending" (quarterly amount
multiplied by 4 for the annual figure). This keeps bill pricing grounded in
recognizable real-economy magnitudes rather than abstract model units.

### Government size

At baseline (G = 270 model units, Y ~= 1142.86 model units), G/Y ~= 23.6%.
This is intentionally a middle ground between European social-democratic states
(general government spending 40-50% of GDP) and the US (~38% of GDP). The
worldbuilding represents a smaller-state economy where social spending is
present but government has not yet expanded to EU norms. Structural reforms
and spending bills can shift this ratio over the session.

### Deferred

Debt-to-GDP normalization is deferred until public debt is modeled. Once a
debt accumulation module is added, the display scale above will provide the
denominator (nominal GDP = Y_pc x POPULATION) needed for the ratio.

## Initial state (quarter 1)

At baseline tax rate, no shocks, CB at neutral:

- Y ≈ 1142.86 (equals Y*)
- a = 80
- C ≈ 722.86 (63% of GDP)
- I = 150 (13% of GDP)
- G = 270 (24% of GDP)
- T ≈ 285.71
- Output gap = 0
- u = 5%
- π = 0.496% per quarter (≈ 2% annualized)
- i = 0.5% per quarter (neutral; i_prev = i_neutral; ≈ 2% annualized)
- scar_factor = 1.0 (no accumulated damage)
- g_current = 0.496% per quarter (baseline; no structural boosts applied yet)
- trend_state = 1.0

## Player levers

The player changes the economy by passing bills, which modify:

- **G** (government spending). Direct multiplier effect on Y — each $1 of G
  generates roughly $2.3 of GDP through the Keynesian multiplier.
- **tax_rate**. Affects both consumption (via disposable income) and
  investment (via the τ sensitivity term). Cuts boost short-run growth but
  reduce tax revenue.
- **g_boost_path** (structural growth reform). Permanently raises g_current,
  accelerating growth of Y*, G, a, and I from the following quarter onward. Small
  per-quarter values (+0.001 to +0.002) compound into large long-run effects.
- **heal_path** (scar healing). Directly restores scar_factor toward 1.0,
  immediately raising Y* and reducing the negative gap that would compound
  further hysteresis. Most effective when applied soon after a recession.

## Per-turn update loop

For each quarter t:

1.  Apply structural policies for this quarter:
    g_current_t = g_current_{t-1} + g_boost_path[t]
    scar_factor = min(1.0, scar_factor + heal_path[t])
2.  Apply this turn's bill effects to G_path[t] and/or tax_rate
    Scale G_path[t] to actual spending: G_t = G_path[t] · trend_t
3.  Compute i_target = i_neutral + phi_pi · (pi_{t-1} - pi_target) + phi_u · (u_natural_state - u_{t-1})
    Select rho = rho_cut (0.9) if i_target < i_{t-1}, else rho = rho_hike (0.8)
    Compute i_t = max(0, rho · i_{t-1} + (1 - rho) · i_target)   [ZLB]
4.  Generate investment shock: ε_t ~ N(0, σ²)
5.  Read demand_shock_path[t] = δ_t, supply_shock_path[t] = σ_t, inflation_shock_path[t] = ζ_t
6.  Compute Y* = Y*_0 · trend_t · (1 + σ_t) · scar_t
7.  Compute a = a_0 · trend_t · (1 + δ_t)
8.  Compute I = I_base_0 · trend_t · (1 − τ · (tax_rate − tax_baseline)) · (1 − d · (i − i_neutral)) · (1 + ε_t) · (1 + δ_t)
9.  Compute Y = (a + I + G) / (1 − mpc · (1 − tax_rate))
10. Compute T = tax_rate · Y, then C = a + mpc · (Y − T)
11. Compute gap = (Y − Y*) / Y*
12. Compute u = u_natural − okun · gap
13. Compute π = π_target + β · gap + ζ_t
14. Update scar_t+1 = scar_t · (1 + min(0, gap) · hysteresis_rate)
15. Update trend_{t+1} = trend_t · (1 + g_current_t)
16. Record state (including scar_t+1, g_current_t); set π_{t}, u_{t}, i_{t} for next quarter's Taylor Rule

## Design notes

**What happens when the player does nothing.** Both a, I, and G grow at trend g
automatically. Y therefore grows in line with Y*, the output gap stays near zero,
and the economy idles at roughly natural unemployment and target inflation. The
player's legislative choices determine whether the economy runs above or below
potential; inaction is a stable baseline, not structural decay.

**Structural reforms compound silently.** A g_boost of +0.001 in quarter 5 barely
shows up in the rates plot — but over 95 remaining quarters it lifts trend_t
meaningfully above the no-reform path. A player who invests in structural reforms
in the first half of the session will outperform a pure demand-side player in the
second half, even if both have the same G and tax_rate. The reverse is also true:
structural reforms provide no short-run demand stimulus — they do not close gaps
or prevent recessions, they only raise the long-run ceiling.

**Why the economy is volatile.** The shock term ε_t means each quarter's
investment lands above or below trend. Most quarters are near-trend, but
occasional bad quarters can compound into recessions and good quarters into booms.
Each playthrough is different.

**Fiscal-monetary tug of war.** Expansionary fiscal policy (higher G or lower
taxes) raises Y, narrows the output gap, and eventually pushes inflation above
target. The CB responds with higher rates in subsequent quarters, which suppresses
investment and partially offsets the fiscal stimulus. The player cannot simply
spend their way to permanent prosperity — sustained stimulus will draw a CB
response that crowds out private investment, though it arrives gradually.

**Recessions trigger easing.** When a demand shock or fiscal tightening drives
the output gap negative, unemployment rises and inflation falls. The CB responds
with rate cuts in subsequent quarters, which boosts investment and provides
automatic stabilisation. Bad luck is self-correcting to a degree, but the
one-quarter lag plus smoothing means the medicine arrives slowly after the pain.

**Single-quarter shocks recover quickly.** A one-quarter demand or supply shock
typically closes within two to three quarters as the CB adjusts and investment
rebounds. To produce a multi-quarter recession — a 2008-style crisis or a pandemic
— apply the shock across several consecutive quarters. The sustained gap between
actual and potential output then compounds the CB response and prolongs recovery.

**Stimulus that overheats triggers tightening.** A large spending bill in quarter t
may push Y well above Y*. Inflation rises. The CB begins raising rates in
quarter t+1, closing 20% of the gap toward its Taylor target (rho_hike = 0.8).
Tightening accumulates over several quarters. The net multi-quarter effect of
fiscal stimulus is therefore smaller than the single-quarter Keynesian multiplier
implies — and the drag arrives on a delay.

**Hysteresis compounds slow recoveries.** Each quarter the output gap stays
negative, scar_factor ticks down and Y* is permanently reduced. A two-quarter
recession with hysteresis_rate = 0.10 and average gap of -0.05 leaves
scar_factor at roughly 0.99 — a 1% permanent loss in potential. That sounds
small, but it compounds: an eight-quarter slump with the same gap drops scar_factor
to ~0.975. The CB's slow easing (rho_cut = 0.9: only 10% per quarter toward
lower rates) means the economy can stay below potential for many quarters after
an initial shock, accumulating scar damage long after the original cause is resolved.

**The CB is asymmetrically slow.** The CB closes 20% of the gap toward its
Taylor target when tightening (rho_hike = 0.8) but only 10% per quarter when
easing (rho_cut = 0.9). A single inflationary quarter barely shifts rates; sustained
overheating compounds into significant tightening over time. In recession, the
medicine arrives even more slowly than the tightening did — the CB is reluctant
to commit to lower rates until inflation is clearly under control. Players have
more room to run the economy hot than a symmetric CB would allow, but rate
relief during a downturn is also slower to materialise.

**Hawkish bias and inflation-targeting asymmetry.** The combination of a lower
phi_u (0.25 vs. the symmetric 0.5) and slower easing (rho_cut = 0.9) models a
Volcker / pre-2008 Fed posture: price stability is the primary mandate, and
employment is a secondary concern. The CB will not sacrifice inflation credibility
to ease unemployment quickly. In practice this means: (1) the CB's reaction
function is dominated by the inflation gap, not the unemployment gap; (2) when
both unemployment is high and inflation is below target, the CB still eases slowly;
and (3) a supply shock that raises both inflation and unemployment creates a
genuine policy dilemma — the CB leans toward fighting inflation even at the cost
of a prolonged slump.

**Zero lower bound makes fiscal policy decisive.** In a deep or prolonged
recession the CB rate reaches zero and stays there. The Taylor Rule still
computes a negative i_target, but it cannot be implemented. This creates a
qualitatively different regime: monetary policy is exhausted, and only the
player's spending and tax decisions can lift output. Hysteresis compounds the
problem — while i_t is stuck at zero, the output gap persists longer, scar damage
accumulates faster, and the economy's potential output shrinks quarter by quarter.
The player faces genuine stakes: fiscal inaction at the ZLB is not neutral; it
leaves scarring damage that lowers the baseline permanently.
