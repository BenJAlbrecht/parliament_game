# Game Economy — v2 Specification

## Overview

The economy simulates a closed national economy each turn (1 turn = 1 year).
It tracks GDP and its three components, plus unemployment, inflation, and the
central bank interest rate. Player actions (passing bills) change government
spending and tax rates, which propagate through the model to produce yearly
economic outcomes. The central bank responds to those outcomes with a one-year
lag, adjusting the interest rate according to a Taylor Rule.

The model is built from four classical macro relationships:
- The Keynesian cross (GDP from aggregate demand)
- Okun's law (output gap → unemployment)
- The Phillips curve (output gap → inflation)
- The Taylor Rule (inflation and unemployment → central bank rate)

Plus a stochastic shock to private investment (business-sentiment variability),
three exogenous shock vectors that can represent crises, supply disruptions, or
cost pressures, and two structural policy levers that raise trend growth or heal
accumulated scarring over time.

## Identity and equations

GDP is the sum of consumption, investment, and government spending:

    Y = C + I + G

Each component:

    C = a + mpc · (Y − T)
    I = I_base · trend_t · (1 − τ · (tax_rate − tax_baseline)) ·
                           (1 − d · (i − i_neutral)) · (1 + ε_t) · (1 + δ_t)
    G = G_path[t] · trend_t
    T = tax_rate · Y

Where:
- a = a_0 · trend_t · (1 + δ_t) is trend-scaled autonomous consumption,
  also affected by demand shocks
- trend_t is a cumulative trend factor that starts at 1 in year 1 and compounds
  each year at the current growth rate: trend_{t+1} = trend_t · (1 + g_current_t).
  With no structural boosts, trend_t = (1 + g)^(t−1). Structural growth reforms
  raise g_current permanently, so trend accelerates from the year after each reform.
- ε_t ~ N(0, σ²) is the investment shock for year t
- δ_t = demand_shock_path[t] is a demand shock applied to both a and I
- i is the central bank interest rate set by the Taylor Rule (see below)
- G_path[t] is the player's real (year-1-equivalent) spending choice for year t

Y is solved analytically from these (since C depends on Y):

    Y = (a + I + G) / (1 − mpc · (1 − tax_rate))

## Mandatory vs. discretionary spending

G_path[t] represents the government's real spending level for year t, expressed
in year-1-equivalent terms. The model automatically scales it up by trend growth:

    G_t = G_path[t] · trend_t

This reflects how most government spending is mandatory or entitlement-driven —
pensions, healthcare, statutory benefits — and grows automatically with
population aging, indexation, and demographic pressures, roughly tracking the
economy's trend growth rate. The player does not need to legislate to maintain
this baseline; it is built in.

Bills, therefore, represent real changes to spending priorities above or below
the trend-adjusted baseline. A bill raising G_path from 270 to 285 commits the
government to 5.6% more real spending every year from that point forward, not
a one-time nominal boost.

When the player does nothing (G_path constant), G grows in line with I, a, and
Y*, and the economy stays near potential output. The player's legislative choices
determine whether the economy runs above or below potential — not whether it
survives at all.

**Future extension.** Splitting G into a mandatory component (grows at g
automatically) and a discretionary component (set directly by the player) is
a planned refinement. For now, all spending is treated as real and trend-scaled.

## Output gap, unemployment, inflation

Potential output grows at the trend rate, modified by any supply shock and
accumulated hysteresis scarring:

    Y*_t = Y*_0 · trend_t · (1 + σ_t) · scar_t

where σ_t = supply_shock_path[t] and scar_t is the cumulative scar factor
(see Hysteresis / scarring below). The output gap measures the economy relative
to potential:

    gap = (Y − Y*) / Y*

When positive, the economy runs hot (above potential). When negative, slack
(below potential).

Okun's law translates the output gap into unemployment:

    u = u_natural − okun · gap

A hot economy → unemployment below natural rate. A slack economy → unemployment
above natural rate.

The Phillips curve translates the output gap into inflation, with an optional
cost-push term:

    π = π_target + β · gap + ζ_t

where ζ_t = inflation_shock_path[t]. A hot economy generates above-target
inflation; a slack economy produces below-target inflation or deflation.
Cost-push shocks add to π regardless of the output gap.

## Central bank

The central bank has a dual mandate: keep inflation near π_target (2%) and
unemployment near u_natural (5%). It sets the interest rate each year using
a Taylor Rule with interest rate smoothing, reacting to the previous year's
outcomes with a one-year lag.

First, a Taylor target is computed from last year's inflation and unemployment:

    i_target = i_neutral + φ_π · (π_{t-1} − π_target) + φ_u · (u_natural − u_{t-1})

Then the actual rate moves only part of the way toward that target:

    i_t = ρ · i_{t-1} + (1 − ρ) · i_target

With ρ = 0.8, the CB closes 20% of the gap to the Taylor target each year.
A CB that wanted to raise rates from 2% to 12% would instead move to 4% in
year 1, then 5.6%, then 6.9%, and so on — never jumping to the target in one
step.

The interest rate feeds back into private investment through the sensitivity
parameter d:

    (1 − d · (i − i_neutral))

When i > i_neutral (tightening), this factor is below 1 and suppresses
investment. When i < i_neutral (easing), it is above 1 and boosts investment.

**Initialisation.** For year 1, π_{0} = π_target, u_{0} = u_natural, and
i_{0} = i_neutral, so i_1 = i_neutral. The CB starts at neutral and only
deviates once the economy generates an output gap.

## Shocks

Three exogenous shock vectors run alongside G_path and tax_path, all defaulting
to zero. They are passed as arguments to simulate_economy() and have the same
length as G_path.

### Demand shock (demand_shock_path)

A demand shock represents a simultaneous collapse of private spending intentions
across both households and businesses — a financial crisis, a consumer-confidence
crash, or a broad credit crunch. It applies multiplicatively to both autonomous
consumption and investment:

    a_t = a_0 · (1 + g)^(t−1) · (1 + δ_t)
    I_t = I_base · ... · (1 + ε_t) · (1 + δ_t)

Routing the shock through both a and I is economically honest: a real demand
collapse suppresses both household spending intentions and business investment
plans simultaneously. Hitting only investment would miss the consumer-confidence
channel; hitting only consumption would miss the business-sentiment channel.

**Recession signature:** deflationary slump — Y falls, unemployment rises,
inflation drops below target. The CB responds with rate cuts, but the one-year
lag and smoothing (ρ = 0.8) mean relief arrives slowly.

Example — two-year financial crisis (years 4–5):
    demand_shock_path[4:5] <- -0.15

### Supply shock (supply_shock_path)

A supply shock compresses the economy's productive capacity without directly
cutting private demand — an oil embargo, a pandemic, a natural disaster. It
shifts potential output downward while actual Y may remain elevated, pushing
the output gap positive despite a weakening real economy:

    Y*_t = Y*_0 · trend_t · (1 + σ_t)

**Recession signature:** stagflation — actual Y holds up while Y* falls, so
the output gap stays positive or turns positive. Unemployment may fall even as
the real economy weakens in absolute terms. Inflation rises above target. The CB
faces a dilemma: raising rates to fight inflation tightens an already-strained
economy.

Example — 1970s-style supply compression (years 3–7):
    supply_shock_path[3:7] <- -0.08

### Cost-push shock (inflation_shock_path)

A cost-push shock adds directly to observed inflation without moving the output
gap — an import price spike, a commodity surge, a supply-chain disruption that
raises prices without reducing real output:

    π_t = π_target + β · gap + ζ_t

**Recession signature:** CB dilemma — inflation is above target but the real
economy may be at or below potential. Raising rates to fight cost-push inflation
induces unnecessary unemployment; ignoring it risks unanchored expectations.

Example — three-year commodity price surge (years 2–4):
    inflation_shock_path[2:4] <- 0.03

### Multi-year recessions

A single-year shock recovers quickly: the CB responds in t+1, investment
rebounds in t+2, and the output gap closes within two to three years. To model
a sustained recession, apply the shock across multiple consecutive years:

- 2008-style financial crisis:  demand_shock_path[4:6] <- -0.12
- COVID shock:                  demand_shock_path[1:2] <- -0.20
- 1970s stagflation combo:      supply_shock_path[3:8] <- -0.08
                                inflation_shock_path[3:8] <- 0.05

## Hysteresis / scarring

Prolonged negative output gaps permanently damage potential output — a mechanism
known as hysteresis. Skills atrophy when workers are unemployed for extended
periods. Capital depreciates without reinvestment. Discouraged workers exit the
labour force permanently. The result is a lower structural ceiling for the
economy even after the original shock has passed.

The model tracks this via a stateful scar factor that multiplicatively erodes Y*:

    scar_{t+1} = scar_t · (1 + min(0, gap_t) · hysteresis_rate)

Negative gaps shrink the scar factor; positive gaps (overheating) leave it
unchanged. The ratchet is strictly one-way: Y* can never recover lost ground
through the scar factor alone. Once potential output is permanently reduced,
only structural policy (modelled separately) could reverse it.

**Scarring is gap-based, not shock-based.** The mechanism fires whenever the
output gap is negative, regardless of what caused it. A demand shock scars
directly: Y falls while Y* holds, gap turns negative, scarring accumulates.
A supply shock scars indirectly: Y* falls, inflation rises, the CB tightens,
investment drops, Y eventually falls below the new (lower) Y*, and the negative
gap then scars further. A cost-push shock scars in the same indirect way:
CB tightening to fight above-gap inflation can create a negative gap that scars.

**The 2008 textbook case.** The 2008 financial crisis was a demand-side shock.
GDP recovered within a few years in most economies, but productivity growth
slowed permanently. Economists attribute this to capital underinvestment,
R&D cuts, and long-term unemployment that destroyed human capital. The scar
factor models exactly this channel: the recession itself, not the initial
shock, is what left economies permanently smaller.

**Gameplay implication.** Aggressive recession response — spending to close
negative gaps quickly — has a long-run payoff beyond the immediate stabilisation.
Every year a gap remains negative, scar_factor ticks down and Y* is permanently
reduced, compounding the damage over future turns. Conversely, a player who lets
a recession run its course faces a permanently smaller economy in the back half
of the session. The hysteresis mechanism gives players a reason to care about
speed of recovery, not just eventual recovery.

## Structural policies

Structural policies are long-run supply-side levers — distinct from short-run
demand management via G and tax_rate. They do not shift Y directly; they raise
the economy's ceiling (potential output) over time, so the same level of demand
produces less inflationary pressure and more sustainable growth.

Two mechanisms are available, passed as per-year vectors alongside the shock paths:

### Trend growth boost (g_boost_path)

Each year's value is permanently added to the running trend growth rate g_current:

    g_current_t = g_baseline + sum(g_boost_path[1:t])

From the year after the boost, trend_t compounds at the higher g_current, so all
trend-scaled quantities (Y*, G, a, I) grow faster. The effect is permanent and
cumulative: a reform in year 5 raises the growth rate for all subsequent years.

    trend_{t+1} = trend_t · (1 + g_current_t)

Example bills and magnitudes:

| Reform                          | g_boost  |
|---------------------------------|----------|
| Public Education Investment     | +0.001   |
| Research and Innovation Fund    | +0.002   |
| Infrastructure Investment Act   | +0.001   |
| Immigration Expansion           | +0.0015  |

**Compounding over decades.** Small g_boost values produce large effects over
long horizons. A +0.001 boost held for 30 years raises trend_t by roughly 3%
above the no-reform baseline — equivalent to three extra years of 1% growth.
A +0.002 boost for 30 years adds ~6%. Because the boost is multiplicative and
permanent, early reforms compound longer and are worth more than late reforms.

### Scar healing (heal_path)

Each year's value is added directly to scar_factor, capped at 1.0:

    scar_factor_after_healing = min(1.0, scar_factor + heal_path[t])

Healing is applied at the start of each turn, before Y_star is computed. It
immediately raises Y*, reducing the negative output gap that would otherwise
compound further scarring. At scar_factor = 1.0 (no damage), heal is a no-op.

Example bills and magnitudes:

| Reform                          | heal     |
|---------------------------------|----------|
| Workforce Retraining Initiative | +0.005   |
| Industrial Renewal Program      | +0.010   |
| Capital Investment Tax Credit   | +0.003   |

### Short-run vs. long-run policy

Demand-side tools (G, tax_rate) shift Y immediately but face CB crowding-out
over time. Structural tools shift Y* gradually, raising the ceiling without
triggering the CB. The optimal player strategy combines both: stabilise Y in
the short run with demand policy, invest in structural reforms to raise the
long-run ceiling, and use scar healing to recover from any recession damage
before hysteresis compounds it.

## Parameters

| Parameter      | Value   | Meaning                                                  |
|----------------|---------|----------------------------------------------------------|
| mpc            | 0.75    | Marginal propensity to consume                           |
| a_0            | 80      | Autonomous consumption in year 1 (trend-scaled after)   |
| τ              | 1.5     | Investment sensitivity to tax rate                       |
| tax_baseline   | 0.25    | Reference tax rate (no investment effect at this level)  |
| g              | 0.02    | Baseline trend growth rate; raised permanently by g_boost_path |
| σ (shock_sd)   | 0.03    | Std dev of investment shock                              |
| u_natural      | 0.05    | Natural rate of unemployment                             |
| okun           | 0.5     | Okun coefficient (output gap → unemployment)             |
| π_target       | 0.02    | Target / expected inflation                              |
| β              | 0.5     | Phillips slope (output gap → inflation)                  |
| I_base_0       | 150     | Baseline investment in year 1                            |
| Y*_0           | ≈1142.86| Potential output in year 1 (auto-computed; see below)    |
| i_neutral      | 0.02    | CB neutral interest rate                                 |
| φ_π            | 1.5     | CB response to inflation gap (Taylor Rule)               |
| φ_u            | 0.5     | CB response to unemployment gap (Taylor Rule)            |
| d              | 5       | Investment sensitivity to interest rate deviation        |
| ρ              | 0.8     | Interest rate smoothing (CB moves 20% toward target/yr) |
| hysteresis_rate| 0.10    | Fraction of negative gap that permanently scars Y*       |

**Y*_0 is auto-computed.** It is set equal to the year-1 equilibrium Y
(with no shocks and CB at neutral) so that the initial output gap is exactly
zero. With the defaults above and G_path[1] = 270, tax_path[1] = 0.25:

    Y*_0 = (a_0 + I_base_0 + G_0) / (1 − mpc · (1 − tax_baseline))
         = (80 + 150 + 270) / (1 − 0.75 · 0.75)
         = 500 / 0.4375
         ≈ 1142.86

Override by passing Y_star_0 in the params list if you need a specific value.

## Initial state (year 1)

At baseline tax rate, no shocks, CB at neutral:

- Y ≈ 1142.86 (equals Y*)
- a = 80
- C ≈ 722.86 (63% of GDP)
- I = 150 (13% of GDP)
- G = 270 (24% of GDP)
- T ≈ 285.71
- Output gap = 0
- u = 5%
- π = 2%
- i = 2% (neutral; i_prev = i_neutral)
- scar_factor = 1.0 (no accumulated damage)
- g_current = 2% (baseline; no structural boosts applied yet)
- trend_state = 1.0

## Player levers

The player changes the economy by passing bills, which modify:

- **G** (government spending). Direct multiplier effect on Y — each $1 of G
  generates roughly $2.3 of GDP through the Keynesian multiplier.
- **tax_rate**. Affects both consumption (via disposable income) and
  investment (via the τ sensitivity term). Cuts boost short-run growth but
  reduce tax revenue.
- **g_boost_path** (structural growth reform). Permanently raises g_current,
  accelerating growth of Y*, G, a, and I from the following year onward. Small
  per-year values (+0.001 to +0.002) compound into large long-run effects.
- **heal_path** (scar healing). Directly restores scar_factor toward 1.0,
  immediately raising Y* and reducing the negative gap that would compound
  further hysteresis. Most effective when applied soon after a recession.

## Per-turn update loop

For each year t:

1.  Apply structural policies for this year:
    g_current_t = g_current_{t-1} + g_boost_path[t]
    scar_factor = min(1.0, scar_factor + heal_path[t])
2.  Apply this turn's bill effects to G_path[t] and/or tax_rate
    Scale G_path[t] to actual spending: G_t = G_path[t] · trend_t
3.  Compute i_target = i_neutral + φ_π · (π_{t-1} − π_target) + φ_u · (u_natural − u_{t-1})
    Compute i_t = ρ · i_{t-1} + (1 − ρ) · i_target
4.  Generate investment shock: ε_t ~ N(0, σ²)
5.  Read demand_shock_path[t] = δ_t, supply_shock_path[t] = σ_t, inflation_shock_path[t] = ζ_t
6.  Compute Y* = Y*_0 · trend_t · (1 + σ_t) · scar_t
7.  Compute a = a_0 · trend_t · (1 + δ_t)
8.  Compute I = I_base_0 · trend_t · (1 − τ · (tax_rate − tax_baseline)) · (1 − d · (i − i_neutral)) · (1 + ε_t) · (1 + δ_t)
9.  Compute Y = (a + I + G) / (1 − mpc · (1 − tax_rate))
10. Compute T = tax_rate · Y, then C = a + mpc · (Y − T)
11. Compute gap = (Y − Y*) / Y*
12. Compute u = u_natural − okun · gap
13. Compute π = π_target + β · gap + ζ_t
14. Update scar_t+1 = scar_t · (1 + min(0, gap) · hysteresis_rate)
15. Update trend_{t+1} = trend_t · (1 + g_current_t)
16. Record state (including scar_t+1, g_current_t); set π_{t}, u_{t}, i_{t} for next year's Taylor Rule

## Design notes

**What happens when the player does nothing.** Both a, I, and G grow at trend g
automatically. Y therefore grows in line with Y*, the output gap stays near zero,
and the economy idles at roughly natural unemployment and target inflation. The
player's legislative choices determine whether the economy runs above or below
potential; inaction is a stable baseline, not structural decay.

**Structural reforms compound silently.** A g_boost of +0.001 in year 5 barely
shows up in the rates plot — but over 25 remaining years it lifts trend_t by
roughly 2.5% above the no-reform path. Two reforms (+0.002 total) applied early
lift it by ~5%. The compounding is multiplicative: each subsequent year, trend
grows at the new higher rate. A player who invests in structural reforms in
the first half of the session will outperform a pure demand-side player in the
second half, even if both have the same G and tax_rate. The reverse is also true:
structural reforms provide no short-run demand stimulus — they do not close gaps
or prevent recessions, they only raise the long-run ceiling.

**Why the economy is volatile.** The shock term ε_t means each year's
investment lands above or below trend. Most years are near-trend, but
occasional bad years can compound into recessions and good years into booms.
Each playthrough is different.

**Fiscal-monetary tug of war.** Expansionary fiscal policy (higher G or lower
taxes) raises Y, narrows the output gap, and eventually pushes inflation above
target. The CB responds with higher rates in subsequent years, which suppresses
investment and partially offsets the fiscal stimulus. The player cannot simply
spend their way to permanent prosperity — sustained stimulus will draw a CB
response that crowds out private investment, though it arrives gradually.

**Recessions trigger easing.** When a demand shock or fiscal tightening drives
the output gap negative, unemployment rises and inflation falls. The CB responds
with rate cuts in subsequent years, which boosts investment and provides
automatic stabilisation. Bad luck is self-correcting to a degree, but the
one-year lag plus smoothing means the medicine arrives slowly after the pain.

**Single-year shocks recover quickly.** A one-year demand or supply shock
typically closes within two to three years as the CB adjusts and investment
rebounds. To produce a multi-year recession — a 2008-style crisis or a pandemic
— apply the shock across several consecutive years. The sustained gap between
actual and potential output then compounds the CB response and prolongs recovery.

**Stimulus that overheats triggers tightening.** A large spending bill in year t
may push Y well above Y*. Inflation rises. The CB begins raising rates in
year t+1, but only 20% of the way toward the Taylor target. The tightening
accumulates over several years. The net multi-year effect of fiscal stimulus is
therefore smaller than the single-year Keynesian multiplier implies — and the
drag arrives on a delay.

**Hysteresis compounds slow recoveries.** Each year the output gap stays
negative, scar_factor ticks down and Y* is permanently reduced. A two-year
recession with hysteresis_rate = 0.10 and average gap of −0.05 leaves
scar_factor at roughly 0.99 — a 1% permanent loss in potential. That sounds
small, but it compounds: a five-year slump with the same gap drops scar_factor
to ~0.975. The CB's slow-moving response (ρ = 0.8) means the economy can stay
below potential for many years after an initial shock, accumulating scar damage
long after the original cause is resolved.

**The CB moves slowly.** With ρ = 0.8, the CB is a slow-moving force rather
than an instant corrective. A single inflationary year will barely shift rates;
sustained overheating compounds year on year into significant tightening.
Players have more room to run the economy hot for a term than a fully reactive
CB would allow, but a hot economy that is left unchecked will eventually face
meaningful rate pressure.

## Not yet modeled (future versions)

- Capital accumulation (investment building long-run productive capacity)
- Public debt accumulation and fiscal limits
- International trade (closed economy assumption)
- Adaptive inflation expectations (π_target is currently fixed)
- Threshold crises (debt panic, currency crisis, banking crisis)
